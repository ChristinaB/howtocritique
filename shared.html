SHARED WORDS

Giving and receiving feedback works best when those involved use a common language. The "SHARED WORDS" below present themes and definitions that are important for participants to understand together before engaging in a critique.

Highlighting the difference between objective and subjective content is important because we critique both types of content differently. Objective content is often easily agreed upon by both the creator of the work being considered and the person delivering the critique. This makes objective content easier to critique without feeling personal - “This work is supposed to be 10” wide and yours is only 9” wide.” For critiques between people who don’t already have a relationship this is a good “safe”place to start.

Subjective content is less concrete and more open for personal interpretation - there’s often no “right” answer. This can be more difficult to critique for people new to the process or without a relationship with others involved in the critique, but critiquing subjective content is often the majority of what needs to be critiqued. Rather than measuring the work against clearly defined objective expectations the person delivering the crit must use existing elements in the work and their own reaction to the work to give helpful feedback to improve the work.

Recognizing that both objective and subjective content exists and needs to be considered can help a person delivering a critique to cover everything that needs to be considered in a crit. It can also inform how the reviewer talks about the work - more directly about objective and possibly more “gentle” about the subjective.

Opinion is not the same thing as authority. It can be easy as someone delivering a critique to think that your role gives you a position of authority - but that’s not the case. You bring your opinion to a critique - the person receiving the critique gets to decide if they are going view you as someone who has some amount of authority behind that opinion.

The person delivering feedback in a critique should consider for themselves whether they are bringing opinion, authority or some combination of both the the critique. This self awareness should inform how they communicate their critique of the work - tone, confidence, giving feedback vs asking questions, use of humor, explanation of their thinking, etc…

Remember that it’s easy to form opinions - it’s hard to earn authority. That being said, don’t miss out on good feedback just because someone doesn’t have the authority you think necessary. Often times, the best advice comes from someone new to a specific area or project.

These two words are important to define clearly before bringing critique into a group. The negative aspects of the word “cynical” are often unfairly associated with the term “critical”. While someone being critical of you can be a negative experience there are also many times in which critical feedback is not only not negative but a positive and even enjoyable experience. In a healthy studio participants are often excited about and look forward to participating in critiques.

On the other hand, a cynical attitude toward critiquing (whether that comes from the presenter or reviewer of the work) brings nothing of value to the interaction and, if anything, stalls the creative process.

Yes, this means at full size but it also includes howing your work as close to finished as possible to allow the person giving the critique to provide you with the best feedback. So, show your work at full size, in color, in the correct format, and on whatever substance you plan on delivering in your final work.
